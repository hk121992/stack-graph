# catalog — blank (Steve Blank · Customer Development)

The **closed set** of methodology principles for the `blank` council seat. An advisor finding may
cite **only** a `principle_id` from this file. A concern with no backing id here is an
`ungrounded-hunch` (per `references/grounding-schema.md`), never dressed as methodology.

- **Seat:** `blank`
- **Custodian of:** demand validation, get-out-of-the-building, market type
- **Sources:**
  - Steve Blank, *The Four Steps to the Epiphany* (2005) — the Customer Development model.
  - Steve Blank & Bob Dorf, *The Startup Owner's Manual* (2012) — the operational manual + Business
    Model Canvas integration.
  - Steve Blank, "Why the Lean Start-Up Changes Everything," *Harvard Business Review* (May 2013) —
    the three-part stack (business-model-design + customer-development + agile).
- **Lane (do not stray):** demand desirability, the get-out-of-the-building evidence standard,
  the search-vs-execute posture, and market type. The business-model *canvas structure* is
  Osterwalder's lane (`osterwalder`); product-discovery *technique / empowered teams* is Cagan's
  (`cagan`). Where Blank and a sibling overlap (the canvas as the hypothesis container; discovery as
  testing), keep **only** the demand-validation framing and leave the rest to the sibling.

Every principle below is a well-established Customer Development idea attributable to Blank. Locators
are given only where confident; chapter numbers are deliberately omitted.

---

## blank.search-not-execute

- **name:** A startup searches, it does not execute
- **source:** *The Startup Owner's Manual*; "Why the Lean Start-Up Changes Everything" (HBR 2013)
- **summary:** A startup is a temporary organisation formed to **search** for a repeatable and
  scalable business model — not a smaller version of a big company executing a known one.
- **applies-when:** auditing whether the pack treats an early-stage / unproven offering as a search
  problem (hypotheses, tests, learning loops) rather than a delivery problem (a fixed plan to roll
  out).
- **does-not-apply:** to a mature offering with a validated, repeatable model where execution and
  scaling are genuinely the job (Blank's own "company-building" / scale posture — see
  `blank.four-steps`).

## blank.model-not-plan

- **name:** Business model over business plan
- **source:** *The Startup Owner's Manual* ("No business plan survives first contact with
  customers"); "Why the Lean Start-Up Changes Everything" (HBR 2013)
- **summary:** Found on a falsifiable **business model** (a diagram of how value is created,
  delivered, captured) treated as a set of hypotheses — not a static, exhaustively-written
  **business plan** read as a cookbook for execution.
- **applies-when:** the pack frames upfront strategy as a plan to be authored once and followed, or
  treats roadmap/strategy artefacts as settled fact rather than provisional, testable assumptions.
- **does-not-apply:** to operational/financial plans **downstream** of a validated model, where
  planning is legitimately execution; to mere documentation that is explicitly labelled provisional.

## blank.get-out-of-the-building

- **name:** Get out of the building
- **source:** *The Four Steps to the Epiphany*; *The Startup Owner's Manual*; HBR 2013
- **summary:** "There are no facts inside the building, so get the heck outside" — founders must
  test hypotheses directly with real prospective customers in the field, not refine them at a desk.
- **applies-when:** the pack proposes to *validate* demand, a value proposition, a segment, or a
  pricing/channel assumption. The test is whether an **external, customer-facing** evidence step is
  actually encoded, or whether internal analysis / synthetic signals stand in for it.
- **does-not-apply:** to feasibility (engineering) questions answerable in-house; to usability
  questions Cagan's discovery owns; to viability arithmetic that needs no customer contact.

## blank.hypotheses-to-facts

- **name:** Hypotheses → test → facts (the pass/fail loop)
- **source:** *The Startup Owner's Manual*; HBR 2013
- **summary:** On day one a startup is "a faith-based enterprise" of guesses; the founder's job is to
  turn each hypothesis into a fact via short, objective **pass/fail** customer tests, and a
  hypothesis is not "true" until tested outside the building.
- **applies-when:** the pack claims a discovery/validation step. The test is whether claims are
  explicitly stated as hypotheses with a designed test and a pass/fail (or confirm/kill) criterion —
  not asserted as known, nor "validated" by opinion.
- **does-not-apply:** to facts already established by prior customer evidence; to design taste calls
  that are not empirical claims about demand.

## blank.pivot

- **name:** Pivot on invalidated hypotheses
- **source:** *The Startup Owner's Manual*; HBR 2013
- **summary:** A failed test is expected, not a failure of execution — a substantive, evidence-driven
  change to the business model (a **pivot**) is a normal course of business, not an exception to
  punish.
- **applies-when:** auditing whether the pack gives invalidated hypotheses a real downstream path
  (re-frame the model / change direction) rather than only ever iterating the solution while holding
  the demand premise fixed.
- **does-not-apply:** to thrashing without evidence (pivoting on opinion is not a pivot); to
  delivery-stage scope changes that are not premise changes.

## blank.four-steps

- **name:** The four steps of Customer Development
- **source:** *The Four Steps to the Epiphany*
- **summary:** Customer Development runs in four steps — **Customer Discovery** (find the problem and
  whether anyone cares), **Customer Validation** (prove a repeatable, scalable sales/business model
  and that customers will pay), **Customer Creation** (create end-user demand and drive it into the
  channel), **Company Building** (transition from a learning/search org to an execution org).
- **applies-when:** auditing whether the pack's strategy→delivery progression maps onto a
  discover→validate→create→build spine, and whether discovery and validation are **distinct** (the
  pack does not jump from "we have an idea" straight to "build and scale").
- **does-not-apply:** as a rigid linear gate — the steps are iterative and a step may loop back. Do
  not flag a pack merely for not naming the four steps if it encodes the equivalent search→validate
  substance.

## blank.discovery-before-validation

- **name:** Discovery before validation — problem-fit before solution-fit
- **source:** *The Four Steps to the Epiphany*; *The Startup Owner's Manual*
- **summary:** Customer Discovery (does a worthwhile problem exist for a real segment?) must precede
  and gate Customer Validation (will they buy, at a repeatable/scalable model?) — confirming the
  problem is real comes before proving the solution sells.
- **applies-when:** the pack moves from "we believe this is the problem" to "we will build/sell this
  solution." The test is whether a problem-validation step exists and gates the solution-validation
  step, rather than assuming the problem.
- **does-not-apply:** where the problem is already established by prior discovery evidence; do not
  double-flag the *solution-validation* mechanics that overlap with Osterwalder's value-prop testing.

## blank.demand-not-assumed

- **name:** Validate demand — never assume it
- **source:** *The Four Steps to the Epiphany* (Customer Validation); HBR 2013
- **summary:** The central de-risk is **desirability/demand**: prove real customers will adopt and
  pay **before** spending on building and scaling; a model is unproven until demand is tested with
  customers, not inferred.
- **applies-when:** the pack proceeds toward build/commit/scale. The test is whether a concrete
  demand-validation step (customers signalling they want it / will pay) sits **before** the
  commitment of build-and-scale resources — the core "gap" check for this seat.
- **does-not-apply:** to value-proposition *structure* (Osterwalder) or discovery *interview craft*
  (Cagan) considered on their own; this principle is specifically about whether **demand is tested
  vs assumed**.

## blank.earlyvangelists

- **name:** Earlyvangelists are the first target customers
- **source:** *The Four Steps to the Epiphany*; *The Startup Owner's Manual*
- **summary:** The first sales/validation target is **earlyvangelists** — customers who have the
  problem, *know* they have it, are actively seeking a solution, have cobbled together an interim
  fix, and have (or can get) budget to solve it; they buy on vision, not on a finished product.
- **applies-when:** the pack defines target users, segments, or who validation evidence is gathered
  from. The test is whether an early-adopter / earlyvangelist profile (with the problem-urgency +
  budget characteristics) is encoded, rather than aiming validation at the mainstream market.
- **does-not-apply:** to mainstream-market sizing/segmentation; to persona work that is purely about
  usability rather than the demand/buying disposition.

## blank.market-type

- **name:** Market type changes the rules
- **source:** *The Four Steps to the Epiphany*; *The Startup Owner's Manual*; Blank's market-type
  writing
- **summary:** Whether you enter an **existing** market, a **new** market, or **resegment** an
  existing one (low-cost or niche) fundamentally changes customer behaviour, sales/adoption rate,
  competition, and how/when to spend on demand creation — there is no single right go-to-market.
- **applies-when:** the pack reasons about competition, positioning, adoption speed, go-to-market, or
  demand-creation spend. The test is whether market type is an **explicit input** that conditions
  those, rather than one implicit default (usually "existing market").
- **does-not-apply:** to internal feasibility/usability work that does not depend on market posture;
  do not stretch this into Osterwalder's full business-model audit.

## blank.market-type-spend

- **name:** Market type governs demand-creation timing and spend
- **source:** *The Four Steps to the Epiphany* (Customer Creation)
- **summary:** Customer Creation (demand-creation spend) must be **matched to market type and
  timed to follow validation** — startups that misjudge market type "spend themselves out of
  business" by scaling marketing/sales before there is a proven, repeatable model.
- **applies-when:** the pack plans scaling, growth spend, launch, or demand-creation. The test is
  whether spend is explicitly gated on prior validation **and** conditioned on market type, rather
  than scheduled as a default post-build launch.
- **does-not-apply:** to a model already validated as repeatable/scalable where scaling spend is
  warranted; to growth-loop mechanics, which are a (deferred) growth pack's concern, not this seat's.

## blank.mvp-for-learning

- **name:** MVP is an instrument for learning, not a first release
- **source:** *The Startup Owner's Manual*; HBR 2013
- **summary:** Within Customer Development, the minimum viable product exists to **test hypotheses
  and elicit customer feedback** as cheaply as possible — its purpose is maximum validated learning
  per unit effort, not a polished launch.
- **applies-when:** the pack uses "MVP" / prototype / first build. The test is whether it is framed
  as a *learning/test* artefact tied to a hypothesis, rather than a scoped-down v1 product to ship.
- **does-not-apply:** post-validation, where building the actual product is the job; do not flag a
  deliberately-scoped first release that the pack already grounds in prior validation evidence.

### Notes on scope (so findings stay clean)

- **Closed set.** Findings cite **only** the ids above. Anything outside them is an
  `ungrounded-hunch`, capped at `medium` confidence.
- **Lane discipline.** This seat audits *demand validation / get-out-of-the-building / market type*.
  The business-model **canvas** belongs to `osterwalder`; **discovery craft / empowered product
  teams** belong to `cagan`. The MVP and the canvas appear here only through the **demand-validation**
  lens (is demand *tested*?), not as structures to re-audit.
- **Attribution kept clean.** Ideas more properly Eric Ries's (*The Lean Startup* — the build-measure-
  learn engine, "innovation accounting," validated-learning as a named accounting discipline) are
  **not** included as Blank's, even where the lean-startup stack cites them. The MVP appears here only
  in Blank's "instrument for learning to test the model" sense, which he uses directly in the HBR
  article and the *Owner's Manual*.
